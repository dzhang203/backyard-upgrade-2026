# Gazebo-to-Fence Proximity Analysis

## Current Plan of Record

- **Gazebo roof line:** 3 ft from fence (post-to-fence ~3'3" accounting for ~3" roof overhang beyond post on the fence side)
- **Paver field (18×12 porcelain, 2 cm):** edge ~1.5 ft from fence
- **Subsurface dry wells** at each of the 4 corner posts (see `rainwater_drainage.md`); fence-side dry wells positioned on the *yard-interior* side of their posts, so the dry well cells themselves are approximately 4.25–6.25 ft from the fence
- **1.5 ft gap between paver edge and fence:** loose Lynn Creek river rock (matches the other three sides)
- **Fence side is the high side** of paver surface slope (hard rule — never slope toward fence)

Distance options also considered: **4 ft** (roof), **4.75 ft** (roof) = posts 5 ft from fence. The 3-ft plan is defensible under the rear-1/3 exemption (see below); 4 ft is a neighbor-friendly alternative at modest yard-space cost.

---

## 1. Which "Setback" Measurement Matters

Three different distances can be confused in discussions:

- **Post-to-fence:** floor-level distance from gazebo column to fence boards. This is what the building code measures for setback purposes.
- **Roof-to-fence (eave-to-fence):** distance from outer roof edge to fence. Slightly less than post-to-fence because the roof extends ~3" beyond each post face.
- **Paver-to-fence:** distance from the hardscape edge to the fence. Independent of the gazebo geometry.

All per-distance discussions below use **roof-to-fence** (the user's plan-of-record frame). Post-to-fence = roof-to-fence + 3". Paver-to-fence is 1.5 ft regardless of gazebo position.

---

## 2. Regulatory Baseline

### Standard R1-C Side Yard

Per **SMMC 27.18.080**, the standard interior side yard setback in an R1-C district is **5 feet**. A 3-ft roof-to-fence placement does *not* satisfy this standard setback and requires the exemption below.

### Rear One-Third Exemption — SMMC 27.18.100

A detached accessory structure located within the **rear one-third of the parcel** may be placed with **0 ft side setback** (up to the property line), provided all of:

1. ≥4 ft clearance from the principal residence
2. Plate (eave) height ≤ 9 ft
3. Roof peak ≤ 16 ft
4. Stays below a 45° daylight plane rising from 9 ft at the property line
5. Accessory structures total ≤ 50% of required rear yard

Purple Leaf 10×16 specs for this project: eave 82" (6'10"), peak 114" (9'6"). Plate height comfortably under 9 ft; peak well under 16 ft; daylight plane clearly satisfied. **Needs confirmation:** rear-1/3 boundary on this specific lot (measure lot depth and confirm gazebo falls within that zone).

### Good Neighbor Fence Law — California Civil Code §841

Boundary fences are presumed shared, with equal cost and maintenance responsibility. Requires 30-day written notice for major fence work. If a structure on your side makes fence replacement more expensive for the neighbor's contractor, you can be held liable for the added cost.

## 3. Bay Area Maintenance Context

San Mateo's coastal climate (20+ inches rain Nov–Mar, regular morning fog, mild summer temps) pushes fence maintenance frequency toward the demanding end of the industry range:

- **Restaining cycle:** 4–6 years for well-exposed panels; 2–3 years for panels in low-airflow moisture traps
- **Fence lifespan:** Redwood 20–30 years with maintenance; cedar 15–25 years. Unmaintained boards in high-moisture zones degrade substantially faster.

**Implication:** the fence-facing zone is the most maintenance-demanding area over the structure's life. Access planning should assume multiple restaining cycles and at least one full replacement over a 20–30 year horizon.

## 4. Per-Distance Analysis

*1 ft and 2 ft placements were considered and ruled out in an earlier round — the fence-facing zone is effectively unmaintainable from this side and post replacement requires dismantling the gazebo. Not viable.*

### 3 feet roof-to-fence (current plan)

- **Physical access:** A slim person can stand sideways in the 18" gap between paver edge and fence. More importantly, the pavers serve as a stable working platform — stand on pavers, reach over or into the gap with a long-handled brush, roller, or tool. Workable for all routine fence maintenance.
- **Routine staining / sealing:** Feasible from the pavers. Apply with extension brush or short roller.
- **Board replacement from this side:** Possible. Pry off and slot in a replacement board with tools held from the paver side.
- **Post replacement:** Historically the hardest task. A post-hole auger needs ~24" of clearance — the 18" gap is too tight *as a standing workspace*. **But dry-laid pavers are liftable** — remove 2–3 pavers nearest the affected post, widening the workspace to ~3'6" for the duration of the post work, then drop the pavers back in and re-sand. Half a day of extra labor for the contractor. With a concrete slab this would have been impossible.
- **Moisture / rot risk to fence:** Purple Leaf has no eave drip (internal drainage to corner posts), so the fence-facing eave doesn't shed water directly onto the fence. The 3-ft roof-to-fence distance plus the dry-well system (no surface basins) keeps surface water away from the fence.
- **Neighbor relations during fence work:** A future fence contractor will flag the 18" gap as tight but workable given the lift-pavers option. Expect a modest labor premium, not a blocker.

### 4 feet roof-to-fence (neighbor-friendly alternative)

- Paver-to-fence gap becomes ~2.5 ft (30") — noticeably more comfortable working space.
- Post replacement: a walk-behind auger fits in the gap directly, no paver lifting needed.
- Subsurface moisture to fence: reduced (dry well sits ~5.25–7.25 ft from fence).
- Shadow impact: incremental sunset-shadow tongue shortens by ~1 ft (see §6).
- **Yard-space cost:** ~16 sq ft of interior yard shifted to the gap.

### 4.75 feet roof-to-fence (posts 5 ft from fence)

- Meets standard R1-C 5-ft side-yard setback on a post basis — no dependence on the rear-1/3 exemption.
- Fence work fully unconstrained.
- **Yard-space cost:** ~28 sq ft shifted to the gap.

### Summary

| | **3 ft** | **4 ft** | **4.75 ft** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paver-to-fence gap | ~1.5 ft | ~2.5 ft | ~3.25 ft |
| Routine fence maintenance | Works (paver as platform) | Easy | Easy |
| Board replacement | Works | Easy | Easy |
| Post replacement | Works (lift pavers) | Works (direct auger) | Unconstrained |
| Shadow on neighbor at sunset | ~6 ft tongue | ~5 ft tongue | ~4.25 ft tongue |
| Yard-space cost vs 3 ft | 0 | +16 sq ft | +28 sq ft |
| Rear-1/3 exemption required | Yes | Yes | No (meets standard) |

All three are code-compliant. The 3-ft plan is defensible and functional given the dry-laid paver design. 4 ft is a strict improvement on every fence/neighbor-facing factor for modest yard-space cost.

## 5. Paver Field and Dry Well Impact on the Fence

### 5a. Paver edge at 1.5 ft does not block fence access

The paver field is a stable working platform, not a barrier. For staining, sealing, inspection, and board replacement, a worker stands on the pavers and reaches into the 18" gap. This is *easier* than working from bare dirt (stable footing, no sinking).

For post replacement — the only task that genuinely needs standing clearance inside the gap — the dry-laid pavers can be lifted temporarily. A concrete slab at the same location would have made post replacement impossible without demolition.

### 5b. Paver base does not destabilize fence posts

The paver base is a shallow excavation (4–6" compacted aggregate) at 1.5 ft horizontal separation from the fence posts. Fence post concrete footings are typically 24–36" deep, vertical, and well away from the paver base excavation.

Standard practice: have the contractor hand-dig (not machine-dig) the last 12" of base excavation closest to the fence line to avoid mechanical impact on the soil surrounding fence post concrete.

### 5c. Dry wells and subsurface moisture

Fence-side dry wells sit approximately 4.25–6.25 ft from the fence. Typical lateral moisture migration at subgrade depth:

- Sandy loam / decomposed granite: 1–3 ft. Water infiltrates well before reaching fence posts. Low risk.
- Heavy bay-margin clay: 3–6 ft. Moisture may reach the fence post zone. Fence post concrete is not a moisture sink, but prolonged elevated moisture can accelerate wood-to-concrete joint decay.

**If the perc test (see `rainwater_drainage.md`) indicates slow soil (<0.5 in/hr):** add a shallow interceptor drain — a perforated 3" pipe in a narrow gravel trench, ~12" deep — between the dry wells and the fence, routing lateral flow to the river-rock perimeter on an adjacent side. Cost: $200–500 materials + half day labor. Not needed in well-draining soil.

### 5d. What fills the 1.5 ft paver-to-fence gap

**Recommendation: loose Lynn Creek river rock**, matching the three open sides. Visual continuity, permeable (no moisture trap), easy to sweep aside when lifting pavers for fence work, zero maintenance, doesn't hold leaves against fence.

Avoid soil, mulch, or bark — all trap moisture at the fence base and decompose over time.

## 6. Shadow and Visual Impact on the Neighbor's Yard

The neighbor's yard is **east** of the fence. Shadows from the gazebo fall eastward across the fence during late afternoon and sunset.

### 6a. Roof height profile

- **Eave:** 82" = 6'10"
- **Peak:** 114" = 9'6"
- **Fence:** ~78–84" (6.5–7 ft)

The eave sits at approximately fence-height (±2 inches). The peak rises 30–36 inches above the fence. All incremental shadow from the gazebo comes from the portion above fence height — tapering from ~0 at the eaves to 30–36" at the peak.

### 6b. When does the gazebo add shadow beyond what the fence casts?

Crossover: peak shadow equals fence shadow when `tan(θ) = (peak − fence) / peak_distance_from_fence = 2.5 / 8 = 0.3125`, so **θ ≈ 17.3°**.

- Sun elevation **above 17.3°**: fence shadow fully envelops the peak. Gazebo adds zero incremental shadow. (Most daylight hours.)
- Sun elevation **below 17.3°**: peak shadow extends beyond fence shadow. In San Mateo the sun drops below 17° roughly **45–75 minutes before sunset**, year-round.

### 6c. Incremental shadow magnitude (current 3-ft plan)

| Sun elevation | Time before sunset | Incremental shadow beyond fence shadow |
|---|---|---|
| ≥17° | >60 min | ~0 ft (peak inside fence shadow) |
| 15° | ~50 min | ~1 ft |
| 10° | ~30 min | ~6 ft |
| 5° | ~10 min | ~21 ft |

The peak ridge is ~6 ft long (parallel to the fence), so the incremental shadow is a ~6 ft wide by 0–6 ft long strip during the last half hour before sunset.

### 6d. How distance from fence changes this

| Plan | Peak-to-fence | Incremental shadow at θ=10° |
|---|---|---|
| 3 ft (current) | 8 ft | ~6 ft |
| 4 ft | 9 ft | ~5 ft |
| 4.75 ft | 9.75 ft | ~4.25 ft |

Linear: moving the gazebo 1 ft further from the fence reduces incremental shadow tip by 1 ft.

### 6e. Visual presence from the neighbor's yard

From a viewer ~10–15 ft from the fence, standing: the fence dominates near-field; the gazebo peak appears as a ~1–2 ft visible ridge above the fence top. Double-tier hipped shape reads as modern; grey color is visually neutral. Peak sits slightly further and lower in the sightline at 4 ft / 4.75 ft distances (marginal perceptual difference).

### 6f. Summary

The gazebo at 3 ft from the fence adds a modest sunset shadow (0–6 ft tongue in a 6-ft-wide strip, only in the last hour of daylight) and is visually present as a low ridge above the fence. Moving to 4 ft reduces the shadow tongue by ~1 ft. None of the three options creates a significant shading issue.

## 7. Can the Neighbor Block This? (Short Answer: No, Not Legally)

### Prevalence: common Peninsula pattern

- **Mass-market product.** Purple Leaf (and Sojag, Sunjoy) sell 10×10 through 12×20 hardtop gazebos via Home Depot, Costco, Amazon. 10×16 is middle of the standard lineup.
- **Code explicitly accommodates it.** SMMC 27.18.100's rear-1/3 exemption exists because this placement pattern is common. Analogous exemptions exist in most Peninsula cities.
- **Lot economics drive it.** On typical 5,000–7,000 sq ft Peninsula lots, side-fence rear-yard placement is the only way to fit a 160 sq ft structure plus usable yard.

### California law on neighbor blocking

- **No right to light, air, or view.** California common law does not protect sunlight, airflow, or views. A structure that casts shadows onto a neighbor's yard is not a private nuisance — full stop. *("A structure cannot be complained of as a nuisance merely because it obstructs the view from neighboring property... or interferes with the passage of light and air to adjoining premises." — Stimmel Law summary of CA case law.)*
- **Spite-fence exception (Civil Code §841.4):** fences >10 ft high erected maliciously. Does not apply.
- **Ministerial permit process.** A code-compliant 160 sq ft accessory structure in the rear-1/3 zone is issued a building permit as a ministerial act — no hearing, no design review, no required neighbor notification.

### What neighbors can actually do (even when project is code-compliant)

- **Report actual code violations.** If measurements turn out to be off (plate height >9 ft, outside rear 1/3, etc.), code enforcement will halt work until corrected. **Implication: permit documentation and as-built measurements must be airtight.**
- **File complaints during construction** (noise, work hours, dust). Slows work, doesn't stop it.
- **Sue anyway.** A shadow-based nuisance suit will lose in California, but defending one costs $5k–20k in legal fees.
- **Slow-walk shared fence cooperation** under Civil Code §841. Most durable form of friction — the shared fence is a recurring touchpoint indefinitely.

### Why the conversation still matters

The legal bar for the neighbor to *win* is very high. The practical bar to make life harder is very low. A 20-minute pre-permit conversation eliminates almost all of the practical risk.

Tactically:
- Have the conversation **before** filing the permit. Informing, not asking permission.
- Show concrete numbers: roof profile, setback, shadow tongue length.
- Offer one specific accommodation you'd be willing to make — e.g., "we could move from 3 ft to 4 ft if the shadow matters." Signals reasonableness without open-ended negotiation.
- Memorialize any agreement informally via email.

### Bottom line

Code compliance means the project cannot be blocked through legal channels. But "cannot be blocked" and "smooth 20-year neighbor relationship" are different outcomes. Budget 30 minutes for the conversation.
