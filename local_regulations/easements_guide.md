## What Is a Utility Easement?

A utility easement is a **legal right recorded in your property deed** that grants a utility company (PG&E, the city water department, etc.) the right to access a strip of land on your property to install, maintain, and repair underground or overhead utility infrastructure.

**Critical point:** The easement belongs to the utility company. You own the land under it, but the utility company has the legal right to enter that strip and, if necessary, remove anything you've built there — without compensating you.

## Types You May Encounter

| Type | Typical Width | What Runs There |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| Underground electric | 10–20 ft | Power lines from street |
| Gas | 10–20 ft | PG&E gas mains |
| Water/sewer | 20–30+ ft | City water main, sewer |
| Telecom | Varies | AT&T, Comcast buried lines |
| PG&E overhead | 30 ft | Overhead power poles |
| Drainage | Varies | Storm drain systems |

## Can You Build Over an Easement?

**Generally: No, for permanent structures with foundations.**

The city of San Mateo's own Accessory Buildings page states:

> "Accessory buildings shall not be constructed within any recorded easements **without the written approval of the agency who has jurisdiction** (Department of Public Works, PG&E, etc.)."

Categories of what is and isn't allowed:

| Structure | Typically Allowed? |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Buildings with concrete foundations | **No** |
| In-ground pools | **No** |
| Retaining walls | **No** |
| Fences (often with gate requirements) | Sometimes, with permission |
| Removable structures (no permanent footing) | Sometimes, case-by-case |
| Landscaping, grass, ground cover | Usually yes |
| Gazebo on concrete pad within easement | **No** without written permission |

If you build within an easement without permission and the utility company needs to access the line, **they can demolish your structure without compensation**. This also creates title problems when you sell the property — lenders may refuse financing on a home with unpermitted encroachments into easements.

## How to Find Easements on Your Property

### Option 1: Your Title Report or Deed (First Step)
When you purchased the home, you received a title report. Easements are typically listed in Schedule B. This is the quickest place to start.

### Option 2: San Mateo County Property Maps Portal (Free, Online)
Visit [https://gis.smcgov.org/Html5Viewer/?viewer=raster](https://gis.smcgov.org/Html5Viewer/?viewer=raster) and search by address. This shows recorded maps which may include easement boundaries. However, **the county warns that some easements may not appear on recorded maps** — particularly older ones or prescriptive easements.

### Option 3: City of San Mateo Public Works Counter
The city has underground utility maps available at the Public Works counter at City Hall. The Accessory Buildings page specifically references this as a resource for people planning accessory structures:
> "You can determine where easements are located by referring to the underground utility maps located at the public counter."

This is worth doing in person before filing for a permit, especially given the side fence placement.

### Option 4: San Mateo County Recorder (Comprehensive)
The County Recorder's office maintains all recorded easements. You can search by parcel or address at [https://www.smcacre.org/clerk-recorder](https://www.smcacre.org/clerk-recorder).

### Option 5: Hire a Licensed Land Surveyor
Definitive and conclusive — a surveyor will locate all property corners and mark any recorded easements on a physical survey map. Cost: roughly $1,200–$5,500 depending on scope. Overkill for this project unless the other methods find something that needs clarification.

## San Mateo Neighborhood Context (1940s–1960s Lots)

The property at 318 24th Ave was built in 1940. In older San Mateo neighborhoods:

- Utility lines often run through **side yards** (not just the front) as part of original infrastructure layouts
- **PG&E gas or electric service laterals** may run along the side fence from the street to the house
- **Water service laterals** typically run from the front, but older homes may have unusual routing
- **Drainage easements** are common in flat areas like San Mateo (near the Bay)

The **side fence placement of the gazebo is the highest-risk location** for running into a utility easement. The back fence is slightly lower risk.

---

## What to Do Before Finalizing Placement

1. **Pull your title report** and look at Schedule B for any listed easements
2. **Visit City Hall Public Works counter** and ask for the underground utility map for your parcel
3. **Call 811** after pre-marking your dig area — this marks live lines but does not tell you about recorded easements (you need the deed/title for those)
4. If no easements are found: proceed with permit application and anchor work as planned
5. If an easement is found along the side fence:
   - Contact the utility agency (PG&E: 1-800-743-5000; City Public Works: (650) 522-7300)
   - Request written approval or encroachment agreement
   - OR adjust gazebo placement to clear the easement width
   - A **surface-mounted anchor on an existing concrete pad** (no new digging) may avoid the issue if the concrete is already outside the easement

---

## Consequences of Ignoring an Easement

- Utility company can demolish the structure with no compensation to you
- You remain liable for removal costs and any repair costs for utility damage
- The city building permit process should catch this (the permit application requires distances to property lines and the city will check against records), but it may not catch all unrecorded or prescriptive easements
- Creates a title cloud that complicates any future refinancing or sale
