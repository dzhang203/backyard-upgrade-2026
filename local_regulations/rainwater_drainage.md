# Gazebo Rainwater Drainage — Design Notes

**Gazebo:** Purple Leaf 10' × 16' Hardtop Gazebo (Aluminum Frame, Galvanized Steel Double Roof, Grey)
**Reference image:** `reference_resources/gazebo_rainwater_drainage.png`

---

## How the Purple Leaf Drainage System Works

The Purple Leaf uses an **internal built-in drainage system** rather than open-eave drip. Rain falls on the sloped double-roof panels, channels through internal gutters toward the four corners of the roof, and drains through a **hole at the top of each corner column** (at roof/eave height, approximately 8–9 feet above the ground). There is no open-eave drip along the perimeter — the eave edge is essentially dry.

**Consequence for fence proximity:** The fence-facing eave being dry is good news — no moisture exposure to the fence from dripping water along the roof edge. The drainage concern instead concentrates at the four corner post locations.

---

## The Two Drainage Problems to Solve

### Problem 1 — The 9-foot free fall
Without any intervention, water exits the roof-level drain hole and free-falls ~9 feet to whatever surface is below. On tile, this creates a forceful concentrated splash at each corner, dispersing water in all directions and potentially splashing debris, furniture, and guests. In heavy rain the volume is significant (see estimates below).

### Problem 2 — Water on an impermeable surface
Below the tile will be a concrete slab. The tile surface is impermeable. Once water reaches tile level — whether via free fall or gently via a rain chain — it must travel across the tile surface to the edge and off into surrounding landscaping. It cannot percolate downward.

---

## Rainfall Volume Estimates

Purple Leaf 10×16 roof footprint = 160 sq ft. Estimated drainage per corner post (25% of total):

| Rain intensity | Total roof runoff | Per corner post |
|----------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Light (0.1"/hr) | ~0.1 gal/min | ~0.02 gal/min |
| Moderate (0.25"/hr, typical Bay Area) | ~0.25 gal/min | ~0.06 gal/min |
| Heavy (1"/hr, Bay Area winter storm) | ~1.0 gal/min | ~0.25 gal/min |
| Very heavy (2"/hr, rare) | ~2.0 gal/min | ~0.5 gal/min |

In the rain intensities most common in San Mateo (moderate to heavy winter storms), each corner post produces a steady but manageable stream. The concern is not flooding — it's concentrated impact and splash at tile level without mitigation.

---

## Solution: Rain Chain + Basin + Sloped Tile

These three elements work together and each solves one piece of the problem. They are complementary, not alternatives.

### Element 1 — Rain Chain (solves Problem 1: the free fall)

A **cup-style rain chain** attaches to the drain hole at the top of each corner column. The chain hangs from that ~9-foot roof-level opening down to tile level. As water enters each cup, it fills and overflows into the next cup below, progressively slowing the water from a single 9-foot free fall into a series of short controlled drops. By the time water reaches the bottom of the chain at tile level, it arrives as a gentle cascade rather than a jet.

**Why cup-style over link-style:** Cup chains are specifically designed for higher flow rates and do a better job containing water within the chain path, reducing lateral spray. Link chains are more decorative but allow more water to escape sideways at each link. For a roof drain (rather than a supplemental accent), cups are more functional.

**Sourcing:** Available at garden centers, Home Depot, Amazon. Budget $30–$80 per chain. You need 4 (one per corner post). Copper chains patina attractively over time; aluminum/steel chains stay neutral grey, complementing the gazebo.

**Installation:** The chain attaches to the drain opening at the roof corner with a simple hook or gutter mount bracket. The bottom hangs freely or is lightly weighted/staked to keep it plumb.

---

### Element 2 — Basin at Tile Level (solves the chain terminus splash)

At the base of each rain chain, at tile level, place a **decorative basin or bowl** on the tile surface directly under the chain. The chain empties into the basin. The basin:
- Absorbs the remaining kinetic energy of the arriving water (no tile-level splash)
- Contains the water briefly before overflowing gently
- Provides a clean, intentional terminus for the chain — it looks designed rather than improvised

**Basin options:**
- Stone or concrete bowl (sturdy, weatherproof, heavy enough not to shift)
- Copper bowl (matches copper rain chain aesthetically, patinas over time)
- Ceramic or cast iron planter-style bowl

Basin diameter: 12–18 inches is adequate for the flow volumes involved. It doesn't need to hold much water — it just needs to absorb impact and allow overflow to flow gently onto the tile surface.

**Important:** The basin should not be sealed or have a closed bottom. It should overflow naturally when full, letting water run across the tile surface to the edge. It is not a collection vessel — it is a splash-break and transition point.

---

### Element 3 — Sloped Tile (solves Problem 2: water on impermeable surface)

The concrete slab below the tile should be poured with a **slight slope: 1–2% grade (⅛"–¼" per foot)**. This is standard practice for any outdoor tile installation and ensures that water arriving at tile level — whether from rain chain basins overflowing or any surface runoff — flows consistently toward the edge rather than pooling anywhere on the surface.

**The goal is not to direct water to a specific point, but to eliminate low spots.** Water should always travel toward a **permeable landscape edge** — the lawn/turf edge, a gravel border, or a planting bed — where it can disperse into the ground. It should not be directed toward another impermeable surface (like the center of the main patio) that would just accumulate it instead.

**Important: the gazebo tile and the main paver patio are part of a single connected surface system.** Both are impermeable; neither solves the other's drainage problem. The entire connected surface area needs a unified, consistent slope toward the nearest permeable landscape edge. The exact direction depends on the layout — the contractor designing the concrete pour should treat both surfaces as one drainage plane and establish a clear outlet point (most likely the lawn/turf edge, based on the design plans).

**The one firm rule for the fence side:** The tile must not slope toward the fence. The fence side should be the high side of the slope, or at worst neutral. This is the only directional constraint specific to this project; the rest of the slope direction should follow wherever the nearest permeable landscape edge is.

**What to ask your contractor:** "Where does all the water from the gazebo tile and paver patio area ultimately drain to?" The answer to that question should drive the slab slope — not any assumed direction.

## Full System Diagram (Described)

```
Rain falls on double roof
        ↓
Internal gutters channel to 4 corners
        ↓
Roof-level drain hole at top of each corner column
        ↓
Cup-style rain chain (hangs ~9 ft, breaks free-fall into cascade)
        ↓
Basin on tile surface (absorbs remaining splash)
        ↓
Basin overflows gently onto sloped tile
        ↓
Water flows across tile to perimeter edge (slope directs away from fence)
        ↓
Off tile edge into surrounding gravel/landscape
```

---

## Fence-Adjacent Corner Summary

The two corner posts on the fence side are approximately **3'4" from the fence** (with 3'0" of eave clearance). The rain chains at those corners should be positioned so their basins sit slightly toward the interior rather than butting against the fence. The fence side should be the **high side** of the tile slope — water should never be directed toward the fence. Beyond that, the exact slope direction should follow the unified drainage plan for the whole patio surface (see Element 3 above). With this arrangement, no meaningful amount of drainage water reaches the fence.

---

## What to Specify to Your Contractor

When discussing the concrete pour and tile installation:

1. **Slab slope:** Pour slab at 1–2% slope (⅛"–¼" per foot) with no pooling low spots; fence side must be the high side. Ask the contractor to identify the intended drainage outlet for the entire connected patio surface before finalizing the slope direction — it should terminate at a permeable landscape edge (lawn, gravel border), not at another impermeable surface.
2. **No penetrations needed:** The drainage system is fully surface-managed — no floor drains required in the slab
3. **Basin placement:** After tile installation, place a 12–18" basin under each corner post's rain chain; no tile modification needed
4. **Corner post clearance:** Leave enough room around each corner post base for the basin to sit flush on the tile without being wedged against the post
