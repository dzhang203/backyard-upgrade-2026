# Backyard Planting Plan — San Mateo

## Design Principles

1. **Variegated theme.** Where possible, plants are variegated to create visual unity across both fences.
2. **Airy, not screened.** Visible fence between plants is part of the composition.
3. **Howard McMinn Manzanitas anchor the back fence.** Two specimens, surrounded by ground-level decoration to soften their bases.
4. **Left fence is medium-height plants only, side-by-side.** No layering, no ground cover, no clusters — just a sequence of variegated medium plants with breathing room between them, plus one accent specimen as a focal point.
5. **River rock ground cover with organic mulch rings around each plant.** Larger 3-ft mulch ring around each manzanita and the existing tree.
6. **Toxicity awareness near play turf.** Toxic plants are flagged; non-toxic alternates provided.

## Toxicity Note & Non-Toxic Alternates

The play turf is adjacent to these beds. Two confirmed plants are mildly toxic: **Iris pallida** (mild–moderate to pets, mild to humans) and **Pittosporum tobira** (mild to pets).

**The honest read:** Both are widely planted in family gardens. Iris toxicity is concentrated in rhizomes (underground); leaves and flowers contain much less. Pittosporum is bitter and unappealing to chew. Pets and children rarely consume enough to cause serious harm.

| Toxic plant | Non-toxic alternate | Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| Iris pallida 'Argentea Variegata' | **Lomandra 'Platinum Beauty'** | Lose the silver-white variegation and blue-purple flower. |
| Pittosporum tobira 'Cream De Mint' | **Lomandra 'Platinum Beauty'** | Lose the broadleaf shrub form. |

***My recommendation:*** Keep both Iris and Pittosporum. Risk is mild. Place them slightly set back from the play turf edge as a precaution.

## Ground Cover: River Rock + Mulch Rings

River rock covers the bed area for visual consistency with the rest of the yard. Each plant gets an organic mulch ring directly around its root zone.

### Mulch ring sizes

| Location | Mulch ring diameter |
|---|---|
| Each Howard McMinn Manzanita | **3 ft** |
| Existing corner tree | **3 ft** (or to drip line if larger) |
| Each medium plant or grouping | ~12–18 inches around the planted area |
| Ground cover plantings | Mulch initially; rock fills in around them as they establish |

### What goes in the rings

- Fine bark mulch or oak leaf litter, 2–3 inches deep
- Clean visual edge between mulch ring and surrounding rock

### River rock specs

- **Size:** 3/4" to 2"
- **Color:** Warm earth tones
- **Depth:** 2–3 inches over base layer

## On Landscape Fabric — A More Nuanced Recommendation

**My earlier blanket "no fabric anywhere" recommendation was overly strong.** Here's the actual research-backed picture, since this matters for matching your existing front yard install:

### Where the consensus condemns fabric

University extension services (Penn State, Illinois, Washington State, NC State) and master gardener programs are unanimous that **landscape fabric is harmful when used under organic mulch in planted beds.** The reasons:

- Mulch decomposes into a soil-like layer *on top* of the fabric. New weed seeds germinate in this layer and root through the fabric, becoming nearly impossible to remove (Penn State Extension's "landscape fabric nightmare" article documents this exactly).
- Earthworms and beneficial soil microbes can't move between mulch and underlying soil, leading to soil compaction.
- The fabric blocks the natural decomposition cycle that builds soil health over time.
- Plants establish surface roots directly under the fabric instead of growing deeply, weakening them long-term.
- Adding new plants requires cutting holes, eventually creating "Swiss cheese" fabric that fails as a weed barrier anyway.

### Where fabric is genuinely useful

The same sources — and landscape professionals — agree fabric **does** have legitimate uses:

- **Under decorative rock/gravel without organic mulch on top.** Earth Craft Landscaping calls this "really the only good application." The fabric prevents rocks from sinking into soil and reduces (but doesn't eliminate) weeds. Because there's no mulch decomposing on top to create a new growing medium, the failure mode of weeds rooting through is much reduced.
- **Under hardscape** — pathways, patios, driveways, retaining walls — for stabilization.
- **Geotextile (woven, permeable) varieties** under rock specifically, where drainage and water permeability are preserved.

### Why this matters for your front yard vs. back yard

If your **front yard has fabric under organic mulch** (the standard installation), the experts would say it's working *despite* the fabric, not because of it. The fabric is gradually causing the soil compaction and weed issues described above — it's just slow enough that you may not have noticed yet, or you're attributing problems to other causes. Penn State's article describes a property whose fabric "probably worked reasonably well for a number of years" before becoming a maintenance problem.

If your **front yard has fabric under rock only** (no mulch on top), this is the one application where the consensus actually allows fabric. It works as designed: rock stabilization plus moderate weed suppression. It will still need replacement in 3–7 years, but it does its job.

### My recommendation for your back yard

**Two viable approaches** — both work. Pick based on your priorities:

**Option A: No fabric, mulch + rock approach (my original recommendation, what I'd do)**
- Plant in soil
- Mulch ring around each plant (organic bark/leaf litter)
- River rock fills the rest of the bed directly on soil

Pros: Healthier soil long-term. Plants thrive. Easy to add new plants or move things. No 5-year fabric replacement project.
Cons: More weeds the first 1–2 years until plants establish and rock layer thickens. You'll hand-pull occasionally.

**Option B: Geotextile fabric under rock only, with cutouts and bare soil under mulch rings**
- Plant in soil with no fabric in the planting hole or mulch ring (so a 2-3 ft circle of bare soil + organic mulch around each plant)
- Lay geotextile fabric in the rock-only areas between plants
- River rock on top of the fabric in those areas

Pros: Less initial weed maintenance. Rock stays cleaner longer. Consistent with your front yard installation if it's rock-only.
Cons: Adds installation complexity (cutting fabric around 40 mulch rings is real work). Eventually needs replacement. Weeds will still grow on top after a few years.

**Both approaches keep fabric out of the actual planting zones, which is the critical point.** The disagreement is only about whether to include fabric in the rock-only zones between plants.

***Subjectively:*** I'd skip the fabric even in rock-only zones because the maintenance saving is real but limited (a few hours of weed-pulling per year), and the long-term simplicity of no fabric to ever replace appeals to me. But if your front yard is fabric-under-rock and you'd like the back yard to match the maintenance regime, Option B is defensible and won't damage your plants as long as the fabric stays out of their root zones.

**The one thing both approaches reject** is fabric covering the entire bed — including under the plants themselves. That's the failure mode the universities describe.

## Establishment Notes

- **First summer is critical.** Drip irrigation with deep, infrequent cycles trains deep roots.
- **Manzanitas hate summer water once established.** After year 1, no summer water on Howard McMinn or Emerald Carpet. Separate irrigation zone or capped emitters.
- **Iris pallida** prefers neutral-to-slightly-alkaline soil. Plant rhizomes at or just above soil surface; do not bury or mulch directly over them.
- **Pittosporum tobira** wants well-drained soil. Won't tolerate standing water.
- **Western Sword Fern** (if chosen as accent) wants moderate moisture for best appearance — irrigation will help. Plant in fall after rains begin for best establishment.
- **Aeonium 'Kiwi'** (if chosen as accent) needs excellent drainage. Plant slightly elevated; don't overwater.
- **Plant in fall (October–December)** if possible.
- **Install order:** plant first → mulch the rings → place river rock around the rings.

## Quick Summary

**Back fence:** Two Howard McMinn manzanita anchors. Variegated Iris pallida and Lomandra 'Platinum Beauty' as the recurring supporting plants. Yerba Buena, Manzanita 'Emerald Carpet', and Silene 'Druett's Variegated' as ground cover at the manzanita bases. One Pittosporum tobira 'Cream De Mint' in the shadier section.

**Left fence:** Three variegated medium plants — Iris pallida, Lomandra 'Platinum Beauty', Pittosporum 'Cream De Mint' — sequenced side-by-side with visible-fence space between groupings. Plus one accent specimen at the midpoint: either Western Sword Fern (recommended) or Aeonium 'Kiwi'.

**Variegated theme:** Iris pallida (silver-white) + Lomandra 'Platinum Beauty' (cream-green) + Pittosporum 'Cream De Mint' (cream-edged) work as a chord in three keys. Solid-color accent (sword fern or aeonium) gives the eye a place to rest. Manzanita anchors and ground cover provide solid-color counterweight to keep the variegation from feeling overwhelming.

**Toxicity:** Two mild toxics (Iris, Pittosporum). Risk is low; both stay in the plan.

**Landscape fabric:** Don't use under planting zones. Optional under rock-only zones if it matches your existing yard system. The plan works either way.
