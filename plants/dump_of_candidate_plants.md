Requirements:
* Drought-tolerant
* Shade tolerant (large nearby oaks will block sun during about half the day)
* Variety of textures
* No fruits -- we do not want to attract pests (rats, flies, etc.)

This is a great yard for a layered “California woodland” look. Given your San Mateo climate, afternoon shade from the mature oaks, and your desire for low water + native-looking plants, you have a lot of strong options.

The fence line especially wants **a layered planting of taller screening shrubs, mid-height mounding plants, and lower textural plants** so it feels intentional rather than like a flat hedge.

A good design direction here is **oak woodland / coastal California understory**.

---

## Best tall backdrop shrubs and small tree-form plants (4–10 ft)

These work well directly along the fence as the main visual layer.

### Manzanita (*Arctostaphylos*)

One of the best fits aesthetically.

* sculptural branching
* smooth reddish bark
* evergreen
* very low water once established
* looks extremely California-native

Excellent along fences because the structure stays beautiful year-round. Some cultivars stay shrub-sized, others can be pruned into small multi-trunk forms. ([GardenUp][1])

Good varieties to brainstorm:

* **Howard McMinn** – especially good in part shade
* **Dr. Hurd** – taller and more architectural
* **Emerald Carpet** – low spreading front layer

This would look excellent on the **right fence and rear fence corners**.

---

### Ceanothus (California lilac)

Probably the best screening shrub for a native look.

* blue spring blooms
* dense evergreen foliage
* fast fill-in
* drought tolerant
* some varieties tolerate partial shade ([Landscaping San Francisco CA][2])

Best forms for fences:

* **Ray Hartman** – tall, upright, semi-tree form
* **Yankee Point** – lower cascading form
* **Concha** – classic deep blue flowers

This gives the yard a softer, lush fence line.

---

### Toyon — use with caution

This is native and beautiful, but it has berries, so it does **not fit your “no fruit” requirement**.

I would skip it.

---

### Western redbud (*Cercis occidentalis*)

A fantastic small native tree look.

* beautiful branching habit
* drought tolerant
* partial shade tolerant
* soft native appearance

It does make seed pods, but not fleshy fruit that attracts rodents in the same way berries do.

Great as an occasional vertical accent in corners. ([Yardcast][3])

---

## Best medium shrubs (2–5 ft)

These are what create visual richness in front of the tall plants.

### Hummingbird sage (*Salvia spathacea*)

Excellent for your conditions.

* native
* thrives in dry shade / part shade
* broad lush foliage
* spreads nicely
* purple-pink spring flowers ([Reddit][4])

This is one of the best plants under oak canopy conditions.

Use in generous drifts.

---

### Coffeeberry — probably skip

Normally a great native shade shrub, but it produces berries, so this conflicts with your no-fruit preference. ([GardenUp][1])

---

### Cleveland sage / black sage

More sun-tolerant, but still can handle part shade in San Mateo.

* silvery-green foliage
* aromatic
* very California look
* low water

Works nicely in brighter sections away from the deepest shade.

---

### California fuchsia (*Epilobium canum*)

Excellent seasonal color.

* low water
* native
* bright red flowers in late summer
* spreads into soft mounds ([Yardcast][3])

Best used in brighter patches, not deepest shade.

## Low front layer plants (1–2 ft)

These make the fence beds feel finished.

### Douglas iris

A nearly perfect choice.

* native
* handles partial shade well
* very low water
* grass-like evergreen foliage
* elegant flowers ([Reddit][4])

I especially like this for the curved edge near the turf.

---

### Alum root / coral bells (*Heuchera*)

Very good under oaks.

* native-looking
* shade tolerant
* soft mounding habit
* good foliage contrast

Great in clusters.

---

### Native sedges (*Carex*)

Excellent for modern-natural texture.

* low water
* shade tolerant
* soft movement
* looks clean against fencing

Examples:

* **Carex divulsa**
* **Carex pansa**
* **Carex tumulicola**

These are ideal to soften the hard fence line. ([Reddit][5])

Great reference point with the star jasmine — you want that same "climbs a wire trellis on a fence" habit, but suited to the shadier, drier conditions on that back fence. Here's what fits:

**Strong candidates:**

**California pipevine (Aristolochia californica)** — This is probably the closest match to what you're describing. It's a true California native, deciduous, twining vine that will climb wires or a trellis. Very low water once established, handles partial shade well (it naturally grows in oak woodland understory, which is essentially your situation with those oaks). Interesting curved pipe-shaped flowers in early spring. No fruit that attracts pests. It's also the host plant for the pipevine swallowtail butterfly, which is a plus if you like butterflies but not a pest concern. **Top pick for your situation.**

**California honeysuckle (Lonicera hispidula)** — Native twining vine, semi-evergreen to evergreen depending on conditions, pink flowers, handles shade and drought. It does produce small berries, so flagging that per your preference. The berries are small and dry enough that they're primarily bird-attracting rather than rodent/insect magnets, but worth noting.

**Chaparral clematis (Clematis lasiantha)** — California native, deciduous, vigorous climber with creamy white flowers in spring followed by wispy, feathery seed heads (not fleshy fruit). Drought-tolerant and handles partial shade, though it does best with some sun. Naturally grows in oak woodland edges. **Strong candidate.**

**Virgin's bower (Clematis ligusticifolia)** — Another native clematis, similar habit to the above but even more vigorous. Masses of small white flowers, same feathery seed heads. Very drought-tolerant. Can be aggressive, so it needs occasional management. Partial shade is fine.

**Worth mentioning but with caveats:**

**Island snapdragon (Gambelia speciosa / Galvezia speciosa)** — California island native, evergreen, red tubular flowers, can be trained to scramble up a fence or trellis though it's more of a sprawler than a true twiner. You'd need to tie it to wires periodically. Low water, tolerates shade. No fruit.

**Roger's Red or other native grapes (Vitis californica 'Roger's Red')** — Stunning fall color, very vigorous native vine, great on fences. *However, it produces grapes — disqualified under your no-fruit rule.*

**What I'd skip:**

Bougainvillea and passion vine (Passiflora) sometimes come up in these conversations, but bougainvillea needs full sun and passion vine produces fruit, so neither works here.

**My assessment:** California pipevine is your best bet — it's almost purpose-built for your exact conditions (oak shade, low water, fence climbing, native). Chaparral clematis would be a strong second option if you want something with showier flowers. You could even do both on different fence sections for variety.

One practical note: unlike star jasmine, both pipevine and the native clematis are deciduous, so you'd see bare stems in winter. If year-round coverage matters on that fence, that's a tradeoff to be aware of. You could underplant with an evergreen shrub (like coffeeberry or ceanothus from the earlier list) so the fence isn't bare in the dormant months.
