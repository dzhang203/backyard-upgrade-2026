## 2. Gazebo Permit Requirements

### Does the gazebo need a permit?

**Yes — almost certainly.**

The City of San Mateo requires a building permit for any accessory structure exceeding **120 square feet** in floor area. The proposed 10'×16' gazebo = **160 sq ft**, which exceeds this threshold.

Key notes:

- San Mateo has adopted the **2022 California Residential Code**, including **Appendix AH (Patio Covers)**
- Pre-manufactured/kit gazebos are NOT exempt simply because they're pre-made — the permit covers the footings/anchoring as well
- The permit process requires: site plan, lot dimensions, distances from all structures to property lines, and product specifications

**Action required:** Submit a building permit application via the City of San Mateo Online Permit Center before installation.

## 3. Side Yard Setback — The Critical Issue

This is the most important regulatory question for the gazebo placement.

### Standard R1-C Side Yard Setback

Per **SMMC 27.18.080**, the minimum interior side yard setback in an R1-B or R1-C district is **5 feet**. This means a 1–2 ft placement from the side fence would normally be a **code violation**.

### The "Rear One-Third" Exemption — SMMC 27.18.100

**This is the key provision that may allow the close placement.**

A detached accessory building located within the **rear one-third of the parcel** is exempt from the interior side and rear yard setback requirements, *provided all of the following conditions are met*:

1. **Separation from the house:** Must maintain at least **4 feet** of open-to-sky clearance from the principal building
2. **Height to plate line:** Must not exceed **9 feet** to the plate (eave) line
3. **Height to roof peak:** Must not exceed **16 feet** to the roof peak
4. **Daylight plane:** Must not extend above a 45-degree plane rising from 9 feet at each side/rear property line
5. **Rear yard coverage:** Accessory buildings shall not occupy more than **50% of the required rear yard**

### Does the proposed gazebo qualify?

**Rear one-third test:** The lot is approximately 0.15 acres (~6,650 sq ft). Assuming a typical lot depth of ~130 feet, the "rear one-third" extends approximately 43 feet from the rear property line. Based on the design plans, the gazebo appears to be sited near the back fence — this is likely (but not guaranteed) within the rear one-third. **You must physically measure this.**

**Height test:** The double-tier hardtop gazebo shown in Gazebo.png appears to be approximately 9–10 ft to the eave. This is right at or slightly above the 9-ft plate line limit. **Confirm the manufacturer's specifications** — if the eave exceeds 9 ft, the rear-third exemption may not apply. Most 10x16 hardtop gazebos have eave heights of 8'6"–9'2"; you need the exact spec sheet.

**Summary table:**


| Scenario                                 | Side Setback Required              |
| ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Gazebo in rear 1/3 AND height ≤ 9' plate | **0 ft** (can go to property line) |
| Gazebo in rear 1/3 BUT height > 9' plate | Standard setback applies: **5 ft** |
| Gazebo NOT in rear 1/3                   | Standard setback applies: **5 ft** |

## 4. Pros & Cons of 1–2 Foot Placement from Side Fence

### Pros

- **Maximizes usable yard space** — avoids sacrificing 3–5 ft of the backyard to setback
- **Privacy** — the fence acts as a natural side "wall" for the gazebo, reducing exposure to the neighbor
- **Visual enclosure** — the back fence + side fence create a cozy sheltered feel
- **Climbing plants** — the fence can host vertical greenery (vines, espalier) as a living wall backdrop

### Cons / Concerns

**Maintenance:**

- 1–2 ft leaves virtually no room to work between the gazebo and the fence
- You won't be able to access the fence from your side for future repairs or painting
- Gazebo curtains, canopy hardware, or the frame itself will be very hard to service from the fence side
- The neighbor will also lose fence access from their side

**Moisture & Rot:**

- The gazebo's roof will shed water toward the fence (eaves drain outward)
- 1–2 ft gap traps moisture, creating conditions for fence rot and mold
- Poor air circulation accelerates wood decay on both the fence and any wooden gazebo components
- Consider: add a drip edge and extend the downslope side away from the fence

**Structural / Safety:**

- Ensure the gazebo anchor points (footings or stakes) don't extend into neighbor's property or any utility easements — check with the city
- Curtain fabric close to a wooden fence = fire risk, especially during summer months (Bay Area fire season). Keep fabric tied back when not in use

**Neighbor Relations:**

- Confirm the side yard boundary is exactly where you think it is — fences are often not precisely on property lines
- If the fence leans or gets replaced, the close gazebo placement complicates the process

**Recommendation:** If the rear-1/3 exemption applies and zero setback is legally allowed, still consider positioning the gazebo with **3–4 feet from the fence** for practical maintenance access and moisture management — this is a small cost in space but avoids significant future headaches.

## 5. Shaded Planter Northwest of the Gazebo

The planter just northwest of the gazebo will face:

- **Shade from the gazebo's hard roof** (direct overhead or near-overhead blockage)
- **Northwest orientation** = afternoon shade (west sun blocked by the gazebo), and essentially no direct morning sun from the east
- **Bay Area microclimate** = cool/foggy summers (already low-light conditions compared to inland)

This planter will likely receive **2–4 hours of indirect or partial sun** at best during summer, and very little in winter.

### Recommended plants for this location:

**Edible / Herbs (if you want to keep it productive):**

- Lettuce, spinach, arugula, chard, kale — all thrive in partial shade + cool Bay Area summers; actually prefer shade in warmer months
- Mint, parsley, cilantro, chives — do well with 3–4 hours of sun
- Avoid: tomatoes, peppers, squash, beans (need 6+ hours)

**Ornamental:**

- Ferns (sword fern, maidenhair fern) — excellent in Bay Area shade
- Coral Bells (Heuchera) — colorful foliage, very shade/drought tolerant
- Impatiens or Begonias (seasonal color)
- Japanese Forest Grass (Hakonechloa) — striking ornamental grass for shade
- Bleeding Heart (Dicentra) — woodland-style, beautiful in dappled shade
- Hydrangeas — tolerate partial shade; consistent with the modern style of the yard

**Bottom line:** If the goal is a productive vegetable bed, the shading makes this challenging. Leafy greens are the realistic option. If aesthetics matter more, native shade plants or ferns will thrive. Consider labeling this a **"cool season greens + herbs" bed** rather than a general vegetable garden.

## 6. Other Important Considerations

### Fire Pit

The existing fire pit appears to remain in place. Verify:

- **San Mateo Municipal Code** and **Bay Area Air Quality Management District (BAAQMD)** rules for open burning — wood-burning fire pits may require specific clearances and may have Spare the Air restrictions on burn days
- Ensure the fire pit maintains **10+ feet** of clearance from the new gazebo curtains and structure (both code and safety)

### Paver Expansion

- Ground-level pavers (under 30" above grade) generally **do not require a building permit** in San Mateo
- Still must comply with Planning Dept. impervious surface regulations — San Mateo limits the total impervious coverage on a lot (typically 50% in R1 zones)
- Confirm the added paver area does not push the lot over this threshold

### Utility Easements

- Before installing the gazebo (with footings), call **811 (Dig Safe)** to have utilities marked
- Check your title report for any recorded utility easements along the side fence — these could prohibit structures within a certain distance

### HOA / Deed Restrictions

- No indication of an HOA for this address, but verify there are no deed restrictions that would affect gazebo placement or height

### Building Permit Process

- Apply at: [City of San Mateo Online Permit Center](https://www.cityofsanmateo.org/4294/Online-Permit-Center)
- Contact: Building Division (650) 522-7172 / Planning Division (650) 522-7202

## 7. Summary Action Checklist

- **Measure rear 1/3:** Determine lot depth, calculate the rear 1/3 boundary. Confirm gazebo falls within it.
- **Confirm R1 subzone:** Verify R1-C (or A/B) with Planning to know if setback is 5 ft or 7 ft if rear-1/3 exemption doesn't apply.
- **Get gazebo spec sheet:** Confirm eave/plate height ≤ 9 ft and peak ≤ 16 ft for the specific product.
- **Apply for building permit** (required — gazebo is 160 sq ft > 120 sq ft threshold).
- **Call 811** before digging any footings or anchor posts.
- **Check for utility easements** on title report or with city.
- **Consider spacing** the gazebo 3–4 ft from fence (even if zero setback is legal) for maintenance/moisture reasons.
- **Confirm fire pit distance** from new gazebo: 10 ft minimum clearance recommended.
- **Impervious surface check:** Verify total paved area stays within the lot's allowed coverage limit.
